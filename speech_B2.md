## Slide 1: The Breach (Lock being hacked)

"What if I told you that right now, somewhere in the world, someone is being paid to do exactly what you're seeing on screen? Passwords being cracked, systems being penetrated, firewalls being bypassed. It looks illegal — it looks like a crime in progress. But here's the twist: it's completely authorized. The company asked for this. They're paying for it. Welcome to the world of ethical hacking."

---

## Slide 2: The Money (Scale balance)

"Now, let's talk about what really gets people's attention — money. Watch this ticker climb. Now look at the scale. On one side: 150,000 dollars — what a company pays an ethical hacker. On the other: 4.45 million dollars — the average cost of a single data breach. Reputation destroyed, lawsuits filed, customers lost. The scale tips dramatically. For every dollar invested in a hacker, companies save thirty. It's arguably the highest-ROI investment in modern business."

---

## Slide 3: The Pentagon (USA fortress breached)

"In 2016, the most powerful military organization on Earth did something unprecedented. The US Department of Defense publicly announced: 'We want you to hack us.' Not their enemies — their own citizens. 1,410 people took them up on the offer. And in a mere 13 minutes, the first vulnerability had already been identified. By the time the program concluded, 138 critical security flaws had been exposed — flaws that had gone undetected by their own internal teams. The entire operation cost 150,000 dollars. A conventional security audit? Over a million, with no guarantee of the same coverage."

---

## Slide 4: How They Work (Live terminal)

"What you're watching now is a live penetration test. Real commands, real methodology. The hacker starts with nmap — scanning the target for open ports and services. They discover SSH, HTTP, a vulnerable HTTPS service. Then they probe deeper with nikto — directory indexing found, SQL injection possible. Now the exploit: sqlmap confirms the vulnerability, dumps the database structure. Three databases exposed. But watch what happens next — instead of exfiltrating data, they generate a professional report. Fourteen vulnerabilities documented, severity classified, remediation recommended, sent to the client. That's the line between criminal and professional."

---

## Slide 5: What Students Learn (Skill tree)

"This is the learning path visualized as a skill tree — and it's not accidental that it looks like a video game. You start at the foundation: Linux systems, Python programming, network architecture. These unlock the intermediate tier: cryptography, SQL injection techniques, firewall evasion, wireless protocol exploitation. And at the apex: malware reverse engineering, full-scope penetration testing, and red team operations.

The field is projected to expand by 33 percent through 2033, with 3.5 million positions unfilled globally. Salaries range from 96,000 to over 220,000 dollars. And perhaps most remarkably, this is one of the few six-figure careers where a formal degree is genuinely optional. What matters is demonstrable skill — not a piece of paper."

---

## Slide 6: Choose Your Path

"And here we arrive at the fundamental choice. Two paths. Identical skills. Radically different outcomes. On the left: criminal hacking. Five or more years in prison. A permanent criminal record. Banned from the internet. A future in ruins. Kevin Mitnick lived this — five years behind bars.

On the right: ethical hacking. Salaries exceeding 220,000 dollars. Location independence. Bug bounties that have made teenagers into millionaires. A career growing faster than almost any other field. The same Kevin Mitnick, after prison, became one of the world's most respected security consultants. Same skills. Different intention. Three and a half million positions are waiting for someone to fill them. The question isn't whether this career exists — it's which side of it you'll choose."

## Presentation Tips

- Use strategic pauses before revealing key statistics
- Employ contrast ("It looks illegal... but it's authorized")
- Vary tone: conversational for storytelling, authoritative for data
- Use open hand gestures when presenting options/ideas
- Make deliberate eye contact during rhetorical questions
- Let the visuals breathe — don't rush to fill silence
